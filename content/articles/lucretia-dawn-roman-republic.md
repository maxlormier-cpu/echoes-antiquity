## A Tragedy Beneath the Seven Hills

In the shadow of Rome's seven hills, toward the end of the 6th century BC, a tragedy is brewing, a drama destined to echo through the ages.

It is a time of transition and tumult, where the foundations of what will become one of the greatest civilizations in history are still forming. At the heart of this turmoil, around the year 509 BC, stands a woman, Lucretia, whose virtue and suffering will become the symbol of a struggle for freedom and justice.

These hills, silent witnesses to Rome's evolution from a simple village to a mighty city, are about to witness a decisive turning point. The story of Lucretia, in this context of the end of the Roman monarchy, forever marks the destiny of the city.

Her life and tragic fate become the spark that ignites the Romans' desire for change, leading to the fall of the monarchy and the establishment of the Republic, a new chapter in Rome's history.

## Lucretia, the Roman Matrona

In ancient Rome, which is gradually spreading its wings toward greatness, stands a figure of incomparable grace: Lucretia, the devoted wife of Lucius Tarquinius Collatinus, a close associate of the king.

Her beauty is such that poets could sing of it for centuries, her harmonious features appearing sculpted by the gods themselves. Her eyes reflect the depth of the twilight sky, and her hair, like a cascade of golden threads, frames a face marked by rare gentleness and nobility.

But Lucretia is much more than her outer beauty. Her soul, deeply pious, radiates an inner light that illuminates those around her. Her virtue is recognized by all, a virtue not imposed, but chosen and lived with unwavering sincerity and conviction.

She embodies the Roman ideal of the *matrona*, an exemplary woman, whose moral strength is the pillar of her home.

## Rome Under the Tarquins

It is a time when Rome, still young and vibrant, is under the yoke of kings. Lucius Tarquinius Superbus, the seventh king of Rome, rules the city with an iron fist.

His reign is marked by tyranny and oppression, a stark contrast to the values that Lucretia embodies. His son, Sextus Tarquin, heir to his throne and temperament, is a man of boundless arrogance and cruelty.

He struts through the streets of Rome with a haughtiness that chills the blood, his cold, calculating eyes betraying no trace of humanity. The terror he inspires is such that even the bravest lower their gaze in his presence.

## The Test at Ardea

As the siege of Ardea, a city near Rome, drags on, keeping the warriors away from their homes, a curiosity arises among the sons of King Tarquin the Proud and their companions, including Tarquinius Collatinus.

Driven by a desire to know the true nature of their wives' fidelity, they decide to return incognito to Rome. There, they find a striking contrast: while the king's daughters-in-law indulge in the pleasures of a lavish feast, Lucretia, in the modesty of her home, devotes herself to the ancestral art of weaving.

Under her agile fingers, the wool comes to life, forming patterns that evoke legends of gods and heroes, each thread telling a story of courage and virtue.

This scene of domestic devotion, illuminated by the soft glow of the moon, captivates the attention of Sextus Tarquin. Consumed by forbidden desire, he watches Lucretia, her beauty enhanced by the nocturnal light. His covetousness, like a dark shadow, grows with each moment spent observing his host, turning admiration into obsession.

## The Crime of Sextus Tarquin

A few nights later, under a starry sky where constellations seem to tell stories of tragic fates, Sextus crosses the threshold of Lucretia's house with an unspeakable intention.

The pale moonlight filtering through the windows reveals his face, tormented by a mix of desire and madness. He approaches Lucretia with honeyed words, trying to seduce her with promises and flattery, but the virtuous Lucretia remains unshakable in her refusal.

Faced with this resistance, Sextus's obsession turns into a sinister threat, and in an act of brutality, he violates the sacred laws of hospitality and human dignity.

Lucretia's chamber, once a sanctuary of tranquility and serenity, tragically transforms into a theater of unspeakable horror. Sextus Tarquin, blinded by his depraved desire and a sense of omnipotence, imposes himself on Lucretia with a brutality that tears at the very soul of virtue.

Surrounded by the walls of her own home, Lucretia finds herself trapped, facing a malevolent force threatening to engulf everything she cherishes. In his desperate act, Sextus wields an even darker threat, a diabolical plan revealing the depth of his depravity.

He threatens to kill her if she does not yield to him and, in a pinnacle of infamy, to place a dead slave in her bed, fabricating a scene of adultery that would cover her in eternal shame. This cruel threat breaks Lucretia's last resistance, forcing her into a hopeless situation.

What follows is an act that deeply marks not only Lucretia's fate but also that of an entire nation. The echo of this tragedy, born in the violated intimacy of a chamber, resonates far beyond its walls.

This night of horror crosses the boundaries of the personal to become a cry of revolt against injustice and tyranny. Lucretia's pain and suffering, though tragically personal, become a collective symbol, a powerful catalyst awakening in the hearts of the Romans a burning desire for change.

## Lucretia's Testimony

The heinous act committed that night is a tragic turning point, not only in Lucretia's life but also in the history of Rome.

As dawn breaks, Lucretia, though broken in body and spirit, remains indomitable in her soul. With a resolution drawn from the depths of her being, she makes the momentous decision to reveal the atrocity she has suffered.

Summoning her father, her husband, and their closest friends, including Publius Valerius Publicola and Lucius Junius Brutus, Lucretia, in a narrative imbued with pain and truth, unveils the dark deeds of Sextus.

Her voice, trembling yet clear, recounts not only her own suffering but also the injustice inflicted by a man who believes himself above all reproach. She denounces the infamy.

Lucretia's account, spoken in a voice trembling yet imbued with undeniable strength, resonates far beyond the walls of the chamber where she stands. Her words, laden with raw emotion and a call for justice, are a heart-wrenching cry against the injustice and tyranny gnawing at the heart of Rome.

She implores her kin to avenge her and, in a final act of defiance, Lucretia chooses to make her own life an example.

## The Death That Became a Political Spark

Before the incredulous eyes of her gathered loved ones, Lucretia, bearing an immeasurable pain, performs a final act of resistance and defiance. With tragic determination, she reveals a knife she had carefully hidden in the folds of her dress.

Her hands, trembling yet resolute, brandish the weapon in a manner leaving no doubt about her intentions. In a swift and desperate gesture, she plunges the knife into her heart, a symbolic act as much as a physical one, tearing through the veil of her suffering. She then collapses into her father's arms, her trembling body bearing the mark of her ultimate revolt.

Her last words, spoken with a strength defying her condition, echo in the heavy silence that has fallen over the room: "No unchaste woman shall henceforth live under the pretense of Lucretia's example!"

These words, charged with emotional and moral power, are a testament to her unwavering virtue and her refusal to be silenced by injustice.

## Brutus and the Birth of the Republic

Lucretia's death, far from being a silent end, becomes a powerful symbol, a flame igniting the fire of change in the souls of the Romans. She refuses to let her story be stifled or forgotten, turning her suffering into a standard of struggle against oppression.

News of her sacrifice spreads rapidly, like wildfire, through the cobbled streets and squares of Rome. Each citizen who hears her story is touched by a spark of revolt, a desire to overthrow the chains of tyranny.

Among them is Lucius Junius Brutus, a close friend of Lucretia's family. Consumed by a mix of grief and anger, he rises as a voice of the revolt.

Facing a mourning crowd, he speaks with passionate eloquence, turning Lucretia's tragic story into a call to action. His words, carried by the winds of history, galvanize the Roman people, igniting their hearts with a burning desire for justice and freedom.

It marks the beginning of a revolution, an uprising that will shake the foundations of the Roman monarchy and pave the way for the birth of a new era: the Roman Republic.

Lucretia's spirit, now an eternal symbol of courage and resistance, continues to live in every heart thirsting for justice and freedom.

## Women as Allegories of Freedom

Women often become allegories of freedom and revolution. There was Clelia, and now Lucretia; there will be Marianne and Britannia.

Lucretia belongs to this long political memory. Whether the details are history, legend, or moral narrative shaped by later Rome, her story expresses a central Roman idea: tyranny becomes intolerable when private injustice reveals public corruption.
